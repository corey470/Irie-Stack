# Maintenance

**A Context Stack is not a set-and-forget document.** It's a living artifact. The work is small but real: a few minutes a week, a serious review every quarter.

Here's what to actually do.

---

## The weekly check-in (2 minutes)

At the end of any week where you used the stack heavily, ask yourself:

1. **Was there a session where I had to over-explain something basic about myself, my voice, or my audience?** If yes, that thing belongs in layers 1–3. Add it.
2. **Was there a session where the output was off and I couldn't tell why?** Note the topic. Patterns surface over weeks.
3. **Did I catch myself rewriting the same correction more than once?** That's a signal. Whatever you keep correcting belongs in the "What I refuse to do" list or the banned words list.

That's the whole check-in. Two minutes. Edit the stack while it's fresh.

---

## The quarterly review (20 minutes)

Every three months, sit down with the stack and read it like you've never seen it before. Ask:

**Identity layer:**
- Is the role description still accurate? (Pivots, new products, dropped projects.)
- Are the "refuse to do" rules still rules, or have I started breaking them on purpose? (If you broke them, either re-commit or remove them.)
- Has my working style actually changed?

**Voice layer:**
- Are the samples still recent? Replace anything older than 6 months.
- Read the banned list. Do I still hate all of these? Add new ones I've come to hate.
- Read recent things I wrote that I'm proud of. Do those samples capture *that* energy, or has my voice evolved past what's in the stack?

**Audience layer:**
- Has the audience genuinely shifted? (New tier of customer, new market, new persona within the same business.)
- Does the "what they actually want" still feel true, or have I learned something deeper since I wrote it?
- Has the "proof they need" changed? (What worked at $0 ARR doesn't work at $1M ARR.)

If the review surfaces 3+ meaningful changes in any single layer, consider re-running the bootstrap interview for that layer specifically. Sometimes it's faster to start fresh than to surgically edit.

---

## The signs your stack has drifted

**The output stops sounding like you.** Voice layer needs samples refreshed.

**You find yourself explaining the same context every session anyway.** Something you assumed was in the stack actually isn't. Find the gap.

**The AI keeps making the same wrong assumption.** Add an explicit rule against that assumption to Identity.

**You stop using the stack.** This is the worst sign — and the most common one. It usually means the stack is too long, too rigid, or too aspirational (describing who you want to be rather than who you are). Trim it. Make it honest. Make it match the real you, not the LinkedIn-bio you.

---

## When NOT to update the stack

Resist the urge to edit the stack mid-session because of one bad output. One bad output is noise. A pattern across multiple sessions is signal.

Also resist the urge to keep adding rules. The "What I refuse to do" list should be 3–7 items. If it's 20, the AI is going to ignore most of them. Pick the ones that actually matter.

And resist the urge to add every clever prompt you find online into the stack. The stack is identity, not technique. Techniques live in layer 4 (the task) where they can be used or not used as needed.

## Versioning

If you're inclined toward systems:

```
context-stack/
  current.md          # the live version
  archive/
    2026-01-15.md
    2026-04-02.md
```

Save a snapshot whenever you make a substantial revision. Two reasons:

1. **You'll occasionally regret a change.** Easy to roll back.
2. **The archive is a record of how you've changed.** Reading old versions is a useful exercise — sometimes you've grown, sometimes you've drifted, sometimes you've forgotten something good.

---

## Sharing the stack

If you work with collaborators, contractors, or AI agents that aren't yours, share the stack — at least layers 1–3. It's the fastest possible onboarding document. A new contractor reading your Context Stack learns more about your voice and audience in 5 minutes than they would from any brand brief.

For agents you direct (the Hormozi/Cowork pattern), the stack *is* the brief. You don't write a new prompt for each agent task — you give the agent the stack and a one-line task, same as you would yourself.

---

## The honest truth about maintenance

Most people will fill the stack in once, deploy it, get great output for a few weeks, and then never touch it again. The stack will slowly drift out of date. Output will get blander. They'll blame the AI.

The discipline is small but non-zero: two minutes a week, twenty minutes a quarter. Do that and the stack compounds for years. Skip it and the stack decays in months.

The system rewards the people who treat it like a living document, not a deliverable.
